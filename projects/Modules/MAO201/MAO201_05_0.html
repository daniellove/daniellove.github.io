MĀUI’S FISH AND CANOE

Most of us follow the directions of north, east, south, and west without question. We have learned that up means north, right means east, down – south, left – west.

But for Māori travelling up would be travelling towards the head of the fish, that is, from Auckland to Wellington. Just like we said back in Module Two (MAO102) when different styles of mihi were presented, one is not better or more correct than the other. They are simply different and there’s absolutely nothing wrong with that!

LOCATION

Kei hea ... ? Where is ... ?

Sentence structure – The question

Present location.

Proper noun – in this context a name used for a place e.g. Rotorua.

Kei hea a + tūingoa?

Sentence structure – The answer

Here the answer repeats the same pattern as the question.

Present location.

Proper noun – in this context a name used for a place e.g. Rotorua.

Kei (answer = location) a + tūingoa.

Explanation

In this context a name used for a place e.g. Rotorua.

Both start with ‘Kei’ which signals that you’re talking about the present position.

Both end with ‘a’ + a proper noun.

Hea is used to ask ‘where’ questions. And is replaced by a location in the answer.

Question: Kei hea a Te Whanganui-a-Tara? Where’s Wellington?
Answer: Kei Te Upoko-o-Te-Ika-a-Māui a Te Whanganui-a-Tara. Wellington is at the head of Māui’s fish.
Short answer: Kei Te Upoko-o-Te-Ika. At the head of the fish.

NOTE: Short answers are preferred in this situation, as this is how you would most likely answer such questions, just like you would in English. For example, if someone was to ask you “Where’s Wellington?” you’re more likely to answer “At the bottom of the North Island” instead of the more formal and longer but still correct “Wellington is at the bottom of the North Island”. However, it is still good to know how you would answer in full should the need arise.
