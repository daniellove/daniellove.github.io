Introduction

In Module Four (MAO104) you learned that in pre-European times activities such as fishing and planting crops, as well as when to sail, were governed by the Moon. But that’s just one component! Predicting the weather also influenced when to do these activities. Māori people would look to nature for signs to predict the weather and the climate for the seasons ahead. Nowadays we just listen to the weather report on the news, look on the internet or simply ask Siri on our phones!

DESCRIBING THE WEATHER

Hover over the words that are underlined for further detail.

Sentence Structure

Present tense. Signals that you’re talking about now, the present.

Adjective – describing word.

Kei te + tūāhua.

An example

It’s hot.

Kei te wera.

NOTE: ‘E + tūāhua + ana’.
‘E wera ana’ is another way of saying exactly the same thing. In this course we are going to stick with the ‘Kei te + tūāhua’ sentence structure, but if you already use this other way then by all means keep using that.

SAYING WHAT THE WEATHER IS DOING

Sometimes simply saying for example that the weather is hot is not enough. You might like to say that the Sun is shining. Click on the words that are underlined for further detail.

Sentence Structure

Present tense. Signals that you’re talking about now, the present.

Verb – doing/action word.

Noun – naming word.

Kei te + tūmahi te + tūingoa.

An example

The sun is shining.

Kei te whiti te rā.

NOTE: ‘E + tūmahi + ana te + tūingoa’.
‘E whiti ana te rā’ is another way of saying exactly the same thing. However, in this course we are going to stick with the ‘Kei te + tūmahi + te + tūingoa’ sentence structure, but if you already use this other way then by all means keep using that.

TYPES OF WORDS

During this lesson we have:

  • revised the tense marker ‘kei te’ which indicates present tense
  • learned weather words that fit into the categories of nouns, adjectives and verbs.

Native speakers of any language learn sentence structure through repetition but this is not the case for second language learners unless they are learning a second language through immersion. For this reason it is really important to understand different types of words and how sentences are structured because word order is not the same in English and Māori.

A describing word.

A naming word.

For example, a common mistake when learning Māori is to place the adjective before the noun because this is how we speak in English. For example:

Adjective Noun
big house

But in Māori the noun comes before the adjective.
whare = house, nui = big.

Noun Adjective
house big
