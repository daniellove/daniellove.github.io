Proverbs

You may have noticed in the article you read at the beginning of this lesson that each season has sayings associated with it. These sayings are known as whakataukī, or proverbs.

Whakatauākī are also proverbs but there’s a difference. Whakatauākī are where the person who first said it and the reason why they said it is known. For whakataukī this information is not known.

Whakataukī

Takē kōanga, whakapiri ngahuru.

Te tātarakihi, te pihareinga; ko ngā manu ēnā o Rehua.

Rehua whakaruhi tangata.

Ngā te rā o te waru

Rehua pona nui.

Ngahuru, kura kai, kura tangata.

Translation

Tānerore’s war dance.

Rehua the weakener.

The days of the eight month

Rehua of the big joints.

Meaning

Someone who skips the hard work but is there to enjoy the spoils of a job finished.

The shimmering of the hot air during summer.

These creatures sing when the heat of summer has arrived.

Indicated that food was plentiful in autumn.

Absent at spring, close at autumn/harvest time.

Te haka a Tānerore.

The locust and the cricket are the songbirds of Rehua.

The exhaustion which summer can bring.

The height of summer, when food was often scarce.

Refers to people’s joints sticking out after losing weight during summer.

Harvest time, wealth of food, the wealth of people.
